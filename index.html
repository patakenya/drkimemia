Understanding Non-Communicable Diseases

Learn about the most common NCDs, their symptoms, risk factors, and how to manage them effectively.

Heart Disease

High Risk

Conditions affecting the heart's structure and function, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, and arrhythmias.

Common Symptoms:

  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Shortness of breath
  • Fatigue and weakness

Diabetes

Increasing Prevalence

A chronic condition affecting how your body processes blood sugar (glucose), with Type 1, Type 2, and gestational variants.

Common Symptoms:

  • Increased thirst and urination
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue and blurred vision

Hypertension

Often Undetected

A condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high, often called the "silent killer."

Common Symptoms:

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Headaches in severe cases
  • Shortness of breath, nosebleeds

Stroke

Medical Emergency

Occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, preventing brain tissue from getting oxygen and nutrients.

Common Symptoms:

  • Sudden numbness or weakness
  • Confusion, trouble speaking
  • Severe headache, dizziness

Prevention Guidelines

Taking proactive steps to prevent NCDs can significantly reduce your risk. Follow these evidence-based guidelines for better health.

Lifestyle Modifications

1

Regular Physical Activity

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous activity weekly, plus muscle-strengthening activities twice a week.

2

Healthy Diet

Emphasize fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Limit sodium, added sugars, and saturated fats. Follow a Mediterranean or DASH diet pattern.

3

Tobacco Cessation

Quit smoking and avoid secondhand smoke. Smoking cessation can reduce your risk of heart disease by 50% after one year.

4

Limit Alcohol Consumption

If you drink alcohol, do so in moderation: up to one drink per day for women and up to two drinks per day for men.

5

Stress Management

Practice stress-reduction techniques such as mindfulness, meditation, deep breathing, or yoga. Chronic stress contributes to heart disease and other NCDs.

Frequently Asked Questions

The DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) diet is a flexible and balanced eating plan that helps create a heart-healthy eating style for life. The DASH diet is rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy foods; includes meat, fish, poultry, nuts, and beans; and is limited in sugar-sweetened foods and beverages, red meat, and added fats.

If your blood pressure is normal (less than 120/80 mm Hg), get it checked every year, or more often as your doctor suggests. If your blood pressure is elevated, your doctor may want you to check it more often. Home monitoring can also help keep your blood pressure under control.

Remember the acronym FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services. Other symptoms include sudden numbness, confusion, trouble seeing, difficulty walking, or severe headache.
